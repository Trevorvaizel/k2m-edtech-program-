## Interview #1: Amara (The High Achiever)

**Profile:** 18 years old, scored A- in KCSE, heading to University of Nairobi for Computer Science. Comes from middle-class Nairobi family, mother is an accountant.

### Full Transcript

**Victor:** Amara, congratulations on your results. Tell me about the last time you felt unprepared for something important.

**Amara:** *shifts uncomfortably* Last year, when I joined Form 4, everyone was talking about Python and coding. I realized I was the only one in my friend group who didn't know anything. It was... embarrassing.

**Victor:** What did you do about it?

**Amara:** I looked on YouTube. Searched "learn Python fast." Watched maybe three videos, tried to follow along, got stuck on syntax errors, gave up after two days.

**Victor:** Two days. What stopped you?

**Amara:** *quietly* No one to ask. The video just kept going. I paused and rewound like fifty times, but I didn't know what I was doing wrong. And I couldn't ask my friends—they already knew this stuff.

**Victor:** You said you couldn't ask your friends. Why not?

**Amara:** Because then they'd know I didn't know. *pause* Everyone acts like this tech stuff is obvious. If you admit you're struggling, people look at you like... like you're behind.

**Victor:** When you start university in February, what are you most worried about?

**Amara:** *immediate answer* That everyone else will already know things I don't. That I'll be in a CS class and the lecturer will assume we all understand AI or machine learning or whatever, and I'll be sitting there completely lost.

**Victor:** Last time you felt that way—lost in class—what did you do?

**Amara:** I didn't do anything. I just... pretended I understood. Nodded along. Then went home and stressed about it.

**Victor:** Have you tried to prepare for university at all?

**Amara:** I bought a book. "Introduction to AI" from a bookstore in Nairobi. It's still on my shelf. I read the first chapter, but it was so boring and technical. All definitions, no... I don't know, no reason to care?

**Victor:** So the book's just sitting there.

**Amara:** *laughs uncomfortably* Yeah. I tell my mom I'm reading it.

**Victor:** What would make you actually open that book—or try to learn AI again?

**Amara:** If I knew other people were starting from zero too. If I wasn't the only one who didn't get it. And if someone could just tell me "this part matters, this part doesn't"—like, what do I actually need to know?

---

### Key Insights

**Past Behaviors:**
- YouTube learning: Abandoned after 2 days (syntax errors, no support)
- Bought AI book: Read 1 chapter, now on shelf, lies to mom about reading it
- In-class confusion: Pretends to understand, stresses at home

**Emotional Triggers:**
- Fear of looking stupid ("embarrassing")
- Status anxiety ("behind" / "only one who didn't know")
- Imposter syndrome (pretending, hiding confusion)

**Jobs to Be Done:**
1. "Help me not be the only one who doesn't know"
2. "Tell me what actually matters vs. what I can skip"
3. "Let me learn without admitting I don't know"

**Language to Use in Marketing:**
- "Starting from zero" (her exact phrase)
- "Everyone else already knows" (her fear)
- "What do I actually need to know?" (her question)

## Interview #2: Joseph (The First-Gen Student)

**Profile:** 19 years old, first in family to attend university, heading to Moi University for Business Administration. Rural background, family sold land to pay for fees.

### Full Transcript

**Victor:** Joseph, you're the first in your family going to university. That's significant. Tell me what your parents said when you got your acceptance letter.

**Joseph:** My father cried. My mother called everyone—aunties, uncles, neighbors. They're so proud.

**Victor:** Proud. What are they expecting from this?

**Joseph:** *long pause* Everything. They expect me to come back and... change things. Get a good job in Nairobi. Help my younger siblings. They sold a piece of land to pay for my first year.

**Victor:** How does that feel?

**Joseph:** Heavy. Like I can't fail. But also... *hesitates* I don't know if I'm ready.

**Victor:** Ready for what?

**Joseph:** For what everyone else will know that I don't. Kids from Nairobi schools, international schools—they have laptops, they've traveled, they speak English perfectly. I'm from a small town. My secondary school didn't even have reliable internet.

**Victor:** When's the last time you felt out of place like that?

**Joseph:** Last month. I went to Nairobi for registration, and everyone in the line was talking about things I'd never heard of. One guy was talking about ChatGPT writing his application essays. I didn't even know what ChatGPT was.

**Victor:** What did you do?

**Joseph:** I googled it later on my phone. Tried to use it. But I didn't know what to ask it. I typed "help me with business," and it gave me this long answer that didn't mean anything to me.

**Victor:** So you stopped using it?

**Joseph:** Yeah. Felt like... like it wasn't for people like me.

**Victor:** "People like you."

**Joseph:** People who don't already know tech stuff. It felt like everyone else had instructions I didn't get.

**Victor:** If you could have anything before starting university—any kind of preparation—what would actually help?

**Joseph:** *thinks* I need to not look lost. I need to walk in knowing the same things everyone else knows. And I need... I don't know... proof that I'm not behind? Something I can point to and say "I learned this."

**Victor:** Proof for who?

**Joseph:** My parents. Myself. The other students.

---

### Key Insights

**Past Behaviors:**
- Overheard Nairobi students discussing ChatGPT at registration
- Googled ChatGPT later, tried it, typed "help me with business"
- Got unusable response, quit

**Emotional Triggers:**
- Crushing family pressure (land sold for fees)
- Urban/rural status gap ("kids from Nairobi schools")
- Identity exclusion ("not for people like me")

**Jobs to Be Done:**
1. "Help me know what everyone else already knows"
2. "Give me proof I'm not behind"
3. "Make AI feel like it's for people like me"

**Language to Use in Marketing:**
- "People like me" (identity permission)
- "Instructions I didn't get" (exclusion feeling)
- "Walk in knowing the same things everyone else knows" (his exact goal)

## Interview #3: Wanjiru (The Intimidated Creative)

**Profile:** 18 years old, heading to Kenyatta University for Communication and Media Studies. Creative personality, interested in video and content creation.

### Full Transcript

**Victor:** Wanjiru, you're studying media. Why would AI matter to you?

**Wanjiru:** Everyone keeps saying it's going to change everything. My uncle—he works in advertising—he told me "if you don't learn AI now, you'll be obsolete in five years."

**Victor:** Obsolete. Strong word. What did you do after he said that?

**Wanjiru:** I panicked a little. *laughs* Then I tried to learn. I asked my cousin who's in IT to teach me, but he started talking about algorithms and neural networks and I just... my brain shut off.

**Victor:** Your brain shut off. Tell me more.

**Wanjiru:** It's like when someone speaks to you in Mandarin. You hear the words, but nothing connects. He kept saying "it's simple," but it wasn't simple for me.

**Victor:** What did you say to him?

**Wanjiru:** I said "okay, cool, thanks," and then never asked again. I didn't want him to think I was dumb.

**Victor:** Have you tried learning AI on your own since then?

**Wanjiru:** I watched a TikTok about using AI for content creation. It made it seem easy—just type what you want, get the result. So I tried to make an image for my Instagram. But it looked... wrong. Like, obviously AI-generated and bad. My friend asked "did you use AI for that?" in a way that felt like she was judging me.

**Victor:** So you stopped?

**Wanjiru:** Yeah. I deleted it. I felt like if I'm bad at it, I shouldn't do it at all.

**Victor:** When's the last time you actually learned something new and felt good about it?

**Wanjiru:** *brightens* Last year, I taught myself Adobe Premiere. Edited my first video, posted it, people loved it. That felt amazing.

**Victor:** What made that different from the AI thing?

**Wanjiru:** I could see what I was doing. Like, every button did something visible. And when I messed up, I could see why—the timeline was wrong, the transition was off. With AI, I don't understand *why* it gives me what it gives me. It's just... magic? And bad magic.

**Victor:** If you were going to learn AI before university, what would need to be different?

**Wanjiru:** I'd need it to make sense for what I actually do. Not "here's how neural networks work"—like, show me how this helps me make better videos or write better captions. And I'd need to not feel stupid when I get it wrong.

---

### Key Insights

**Past Behaviors:**
- Uncle's "obsolete" warning triggered panic
- Asked IT cousin, got technical jargon, brain "shut off"
- Tried AI image generation from TikTok tutorial, result was bad
- Friend's judgment made her delete and quit
- Successfully self-taught Adobe Premiere (visible cause-and-effect)

**Emotional Triggers:**
- Fear of obsolescence (uncle's warning)
- Technical language = exclusion
- Social judgment (friend's comment)
- Identity: "creative person, not tech person"

**Jobs to Be Done:**
1. "Show me AI makes sense for what I actually do (creative work)"
2. "Let me fail without feeling stupid"
3. "Help me understand the 'why,' not just the 'how'"

**Language to Use in Marketing:**
- "For what you actually do" (relevance)
- "Bad magic" (her phrase for opaque AI)
- "Make sense" (her need for understanding)

## Interview #4: Brian (The Self-Taught Dabbler)

**Profile:** 19 years old, heading to JKUAT for Software Engineering. Self-taught coder, already uses some AI tools but skeptically.

### Full Transcript

**Victor:** Brian, you're going for Software Engineering. You already know how to code?

**Brian:** Yeah, taught myself Python, JavaScript. Built a few small projects. Nothing huge, but I can make things work.

**Victor:** When did you first use AI tools?

**Brian:** Maybe a year ago? I used GitHub Copilot for a project. It autocompleted my code. I thought it was cool.

**Victor:** Cool. But did you keep using it?

**Brian:** *hesitates* Not really. I mean, sometimes. But half the time it suggested code that didn't actually work, or I didn't understand what it was doing. I'd just delete it and write it myself.

**Victor:** Why not keep the suggestions?

**Brian:** Because if I don't understand it, I can't explain it. And if I can't explain it, I don't really know it, you know?

**Victor:** So even though you're technical, you found AI... frustrating?

**Brian:** *nods* Yeah. Like, it's fast, but it's also kind of dumb? It doesn't know what I'm actually trying to build. It just guesses based on patterns.

**Victor:** When was the last time you tried to use AI for something serious?

**Brian:** Last month. I was stuck on a bug in my code for like three hours. Finally asked ChatGPT. It gave me an answer, but the answer was for a different version of the library I was using. Made things worse.

**Victor:** What did you do?

**Brian:** Went to Stack Overflow. Found a human who explained the actual problem.

**Victor:** So you trust humans more than AI?

**Brian:** I trust humans who understand my context. AI doesn't have context. It just has data.

**Victor:** Starting university soon—how do you think AI will show up there?

**Brian:** Honestly? Everyone's going to use it to cheat. Copy-paste essays, generate code they don't understand, submit it. Professors won't know, or they'll know but won't care.

**Victor:** And you?

**Brian:** I don't want to be the guy who graduated with a degree but can't actually do the work. So I need to figure out how to use AI without it replacing my thinking. But I don't know how to do that yet.

**Victor:** If there was a way to learn that—using AI without losing your edge—would you?

**Brian:** *leans forward* Absolutely. That's the skill I actually need. Not "how to make AI write my code," but "how to use AI to make me better at coding."

---

### Key Insights

**Past Behaviors:**
- Uses GitHub Copilot but deletes suggestions he doesn't understand
- Asked ChatGPT for bug help, got wrong library version, made things worse
- Went to Stack Overflow for human who understood context
- Preference: Human context > AI data

**Emotional Triggers:**
- Pride in understanding ("if I can't explain it, I don't know it")
- Fear of becoming dependent
- Concern about peers cheating and devaluing his real skills
- Wants AI as enhancement, not replacement

**Jobs to Be Done:**
1. "Help me use AI without losing my edge"
2. "Teach me to get context into AI so it's actually useful"
3. "Keep me from becoming someone who can't do the work"

**Language to Use in Marketing:**
- "Use AI without it replacing your thinking" (his goal)
- "AI doesn't have context" (his criticism)
- "Better at coding" not "write code for me" (enhancement frame)

## Interview #5: Faith (The Career Pragmatist)

**Profile:** 18 years old, heading to Strathmore University for Actuarial Science. Career-focused, pragmatic about education as investment.

### Full Transcript

**Victor:** Faith, Actuarial Science. That's competitive. Why that field?

**Faith:** Job security. Good salary. My dad's an accountant, he says actuaries make twice what he does.

**Victor:** Smart. Where does AI fit into that plan?

**Faith:** I keep hearing companies want people who know AI. Like, even in finance. So I figured I should probably learn it before I start looking for internships.

**Victor:** You "figured you should." Have you tried?

**Faith:** I signed up for a free Coursera course two months ago. "AI for Everyone" by Andrew Ng.

**Victor:** How far did you get?

**Faith:** *laughs* Week one. Maybe watched three videos. Then life happened—exams, graduation, family stuff. I kept telling myself I'd go back to it.

**Victor:** But you didn't.

**Faith:** No. It felt like homework. And I just finished school—I didn't want more homework.

**Victor:** What would have made you finish it?

**Faith:** *thinks* If someone was waiting for me to finish. Like, if I knew I'd have to show up somewhere and talk about what I learned, I'd have done it. But it's just me and a video, so...

**Victor:** Accountability.

**Faith:** Yeah. Also, if I knew it would actually matter. Like, would finishing that course *actually* help me get an internship? Or is it just one of a million things people say you "should" do?

**Victor:** Good question. What's the last thing you learned that you *knew* would matter?

**Faith:** Excel. I spent two weeks learning pivot tables and VLOOKUPs because my dad told me "every single company uses Excel, and most people are terrible at it." So I learned it, got good at it. Now I can put that on my CV and actually prove it.

**Victor:** Prove it how?

**Faith:** I did the books for my church's fundraiser. Built a whole budget tracker in Excel. People saw it work. That's proof.

**Victor:** So for AI, you'd need the same thing—proof you can use it, not just a certificate?

**Faith:** Exactly. Certificates are cheap. Everyone has them now. I need something that shows "I actually used this, and here's the result."

**Victor:** Starting university in a few weeks. What's your actual worry about AI?

**Faith:** That it's going to be one more thing I "should" know but don't have time for. And then three years from now, I'll be applying for jobs and realize everyone else figured it out and I didn't.

---

### Key Insights

**Past Behaviors:**
- Signed up for Coursera "AI for Everyone" — quit after Week 1
- Successfully learned Excel in 2 weeks (dad's recommendation + clear job market signal)
- Applied Excel skills to church fundraiser (tangible proof)

**Emotional Triggers:**
- Career pragmatism (job security, salary)
- Accountability gap (alone with video = no completion)
- Certificate skepticism ("certificates are cheap")
- Future regret fear ("everyone else figured it out")

**Jobs to Be Done:**
1. "Give me accountability so I actually finish"
2. "Show me this will actually help me get internships"
3. "Let me build something I can prove, not just a certificate"

**Language to Use in Marketing:**
- "Someone waiting for you to finish" (accountability)
- "Proof you can use it" vs. "certificate"
- "Actually matter" for job market
