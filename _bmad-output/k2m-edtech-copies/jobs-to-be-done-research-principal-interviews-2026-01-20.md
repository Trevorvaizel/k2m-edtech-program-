## Interview #1: Mr. Kibet (The Progressive Innovator)

**Profile:** 55 years old, Principal at mid-tier private secondary school in Nairobi (500 students). 30 years in education. Pragmatic about education as business.

### Full Transcript

**Victor:** Mr. Kibet, tell me about the last time you added a new program to your school.

**Mr. Kibet:** Six months ago, we added a robotics club. I hired an external instructor, bought equipment. Cost about 300,000 shillings to set up.

**Victor:** Why robotics?

**Mr. Kibet:** *straightforward* Parents were asking for it. Three other schools in our area already had robotics programs. If we didn't offer it, we'd look behind.

**Victor:** Did it work?

**Mr. Kibet:** Define "work." We have 25 students enrolled. They built a robot that competed in a regional competition. Parents who signed up are happy. But is it worth 300,000 shillings? *shrugs* It's a marketing tool.

**Victor:** Marketing tool for what?

**Mr. Kibet:** For admissions. When parents tour the school, I can show them the robotics lab. It signals "this school is modern, forward-thinking." Whether the students actually become engineers is beside the point.

**Victor:** That's unusually honest.

**Mr. Kibet:** *laughs* I've been doing this 30 years. Education is a business. Parents choose schools based on perception of value. If other schools offer tech programs and we don't, we lose students.

**Victor:** Have you thought about AI programs?

**Mr. Kibet:** Of course. Every week, some company emails me: "AI curriculum for your school," "Partner with us to teach AI." Most of it is noise.

**Victor:** What would make you actually say yes to an AI program?

**Mr. Kibet:** Four things. One, it has to be affordable—I can't spend 500,000 shillings on something unproven. Two, it has to be easy to implement—I don't have bandwidth for complicated rollouts. Three, it has to make my school look good—something I can talk about in admissions meetings. Four, parents have to want it.

**Victor:** Parents have to want it. How do you know what parents want?

**Mr. Kibet:** They tell me. At parent-teacher meetings, they ask: "Are you teaching AI? Are you teaching coding?" If enough parents ask, I know it matters.

**Victor:** If you brought in an AI program and it worked, what would happen?

**Mr. Kibet:** I'd advertise it. "Our students learn AI before university." I'd put it on our website, our brochures, our Facebook page. It's a differentiator.

---

### Key Insights

**Decision Criteria (In Order):**
1. Affordable (not 500K+ for unproven)
2. Easy to implement (no bandwidth for complexity)
3. Makes school look good (admissions marketing)
4. Parents want it (validated demand signal)

**Past Behavior:**
- 300K robotics club = primarily marketing tool
- Competitive pressure from other schools
- Bombarded by vendor emails ("most is noise")

**Jobs to Be Done:**
1. "Give me something to show parents at admissions tours"
2. "Don't make this complicated to implement"
3. "Help me keep up with competing schools"

**B2B Positioning:**
- Lead with competitive positioning ("3 schools in your area already...")
- Emphasize ease of implementation
- Provide marketing assets (website copy, brochure text)

## Interview #2: Mrs. Achieng (The Budget-Constrained Guardian)

**Profile:** 48 years old, Principal at rural public secondary school (400 students). Limited resources, no budget for "extras." Focused on KCSE pass rates.

### Full Transcript

**Victor:** Mrs. Achieng, what's the biggest challenge you face as a principal?

**Mrs. Achieng:** *exhales* Where do I start? Not enough teachers. Not enough books. Classrooms are overcrowded. And now everyone wants us to teach technology when we barely have computers.

**Victor:** You barely have computers?

**Mrs. Achieng:** We have 20 computers for 400 students. They're old, slow. The internet cuts out every other day. But the ministry sends us a curriculum that assumes every student has a laptop.

**Victor:** When was the last time someone approached you about adding a tech program?

**Mrs. Achieng:** Last year. A company wanted to sell us coding lessons. 200,000 shillings. I said no.

**Victor:** Why?

**Mrs. Achieng:** *firmly* Because I don't have 200,000 shillings for extras. My budget goes to essentials—teacher salaries, chalk, repairs. If I spend money on coding lessons, I'm taking it from something else.

**Victor:** Have parents asked about AI or tech programs?

**Mrs. Achieng:** Not directly. But they ask "Why don't we have what Nairobi schools have?" They see what's happening in the city—robotics, coding clubs—and they feel their children are being left behind.

**Victor:** Do you agree they're being left behind?

**Mrs. Achieng:** *pause* Yes. But I don't know how to fix it. I can't compete with urban schools. They have money, resources, connections. I'm just trying to make sure my students pass KCSE.

**Victor:** If someone offered a tech program that was affordable—really affordable—and easy to implement, would you consider it?

**Mrs. Achieng:** *cautious* Maybe. But I'd need proof it works. I can't experiment with my students' education. If I bring in something and it fails, parents lose trust in me.

**Victor:** What would "proof" look like?

**Mrs. Achieng:** Other schools like mine—rural, public, limited budget—doing it successfully. Not fancy private schools in Nairobi. Schools like mine.

---

### Key Insights

**Infrastructure Reality:**
- 20 computers for 400 students
- Unreliable internet
- Budget = essentials only (salaries, chalk, repairs)
- No 200K for "extras"

**Emotional Reality:**
- Parents compare to Nairobi schools ("why don't we have...")
- Knows students are being left behind
- Can't compete with urban schools
- Focused on KCSE pass rates as primary metric

**Jobs to Be Done:**
1. "Help my students not be left behind"
2. "Don't ask me to spend money I don't have"
3. "Show me schools like mine making it work"

**B2B Positioning:**
- Must be extremely affordable or subsidized
- Proof must come from similar schools (not elite Nairobi)
- Consider mobile-first, low-bandwidth design
- Potential CSR/scholarship angle

## Interview #3: Dr. Ndung'u (The Competitive Positioner)

**Profile:** 52 years old, Principal at top-tier international school (British curriculum, 300 students). Students headed to Oxford, Harvard, Stanford. 1.5M KES/year fees.

### Full Transcript

**Victor:** Dr. Ndung'u, your school is competitive. What's the last program you added to stay ahead?

**Dr. Ndung'u:** This term, we launched an entrepreneurship incubator. Students pitch business ideas, we fund the best ones. It's modeled after programs in UK and US schools.

**Victor:** Why entrepreneurship?

**Dr. Ndung'u:** Because our students are going to elite universities—Oxford, Harvard, Stanford. Those universities want entrepreneurial thinking, not just grades. We're preparing them for that level.

**Victor:** How much did it cost to set up?

**Dr. Ndung'u:** About 1.2 million shillings. But our parents expect premium offerings. They're paying 1.5 million per year in fees—they want world-class education.

**Victor:** Have you thought about AI education?

**Dr. Ndung'u:** We already teach it. Our computer science curriculum includes machine learning, neural networks, Python. We brought in guest lecturers from industry.

**Victor:** So you don't need external programs?

**Dr. Ndung'u:** *pause* We have the technical content. What we don't have is practical application. Students learn the theory, but they're not using AI daily, integrating it into their workflow. That's a gap.

**Victor:** What would fill that gap?

**Dr. Ndung'u:** Something experiential. Not lectures, not textbooks. A program where students use AI for real projects—writing, research, problem-solving. And ideally, something that gives them a credential they can put on their university applications.

**Victor:** Would you pay for that?

**Dr. Ndung'u:** If it's high quality and gives our students an edge, yes. But it has to be internationally recognized. A certificate from a local company won't impress Oxford.

---

### Key Insights

**Context:**
- 1.5M KES/year fees = premium expectations
- Students applying to Oxford, Harvard, Stanford
- Already teaching AI theory (ML, neural networks, Python)
- 1.2M entrepreneurship incubator = normal investment

**Gap Identified:**
- Have theory, lack practical application
- Students not using AI daily
- Need experiential learning, not more lectures

**Decision Criteria:**
- High quality (will pay for it)
- Internationally recognized credential
- Edge for university applications
- Must impress elite admissions

**Jobs to Be Done:**
1. "Give students practical AI application, not just theory"
2. "Provide credentials that impress elite universities"
3. "Maintain our competitive edge over other international schools"

**B2B Positioning:**
- Not a fit for standard K2M positioning
- Would need international partnership/credential
- Potential premium tier with different value proposition

## Interview #4: Mrs. Wambui (The Equity Champion)

**Profile:** 50 years old, Principal at girls' boarding school (300 students). Focus on empowerment, students from difficult backgrounds.

### Full Transcript

**Victor:** Mrs. Wambui, your school focuses on girls' education. Tell me about that mission.

**Mrs. Wambui:** We believe every girl deserves a chance, regardless of background. Many of our students come from difficult circumstances—poverty, broken families. We're not just teaching subjects. We're building confidence.

**Victor:** When was the last time you added a program specifically to build confidence?

**Mrs. Wambui:** Two years ago, we started a mentorship program. Professional women from Nairobi come once a month to mentor students. It's been transformative.

**Victor:** Transformative how?

**Mrs. Wambui:** The girls see possibility. One of our students was mentored by a woman engineer—she's now studying engineering at university. Before that, she didn't think engineering was "for girls."

**Victor:** Have you thought about tech education for your students?

**Mrs. Wambui:** Constantly. Tech is male-dominated. If our girls don't learn it, they'll be locked out of those careers. But...

**Victor:** But?

**Mrs. Wambui:** *frustrated* Most tech programs assume students already have confidence. They teach skills, but they don't address the identity barrier. Our girls say "I'm not good at math" or "Tech is for boys." Until we break that, the skills don't stick.

**Victor:** If there was a program that addressed identity first, before skills, would that interest you?

**Mrs. Wambui:** *leans forward* Absolutely. That's what we need. Not "here's how to code," but "you belong in tech, and here's how to see yourself that way."

**Victor:** What would make you trust it actually works?

**Mrs. Wambui:** If it was designed with girls in mind. Not just "tech for everyone"—specifically for girls who've been told they're not tech people. And if I could see data: how many girls started, how many finished, how many actually changed their beliefs.

---

### Key Insights

**Context:**
- Girls from difficult backgrounds
- Mission: Building confidence, not just teaching subjects
- Mentorship program = transformative (visibility, possibility)
- Recognizes identity barrier in tech ("Tech is for boys")

**Gap Identified:**
- Tech programs assume confidence already exists
- Girls have internalized "I'm not good at math" / "Tech is for boys"
- Skills don't stick without identity shift first

**Decision Criteria:**
- Designed specifically for girls
- Addresses identity before skills
- Data on belief change, not just completion

**Jobs to Be Done:**
1. "Break the identity barrier before teaching skills"
2. "Show my girls they belong in tech"
3. "Give me data on belief change, not just completion"

**B2B Positioning:**
- K2M's identity-first approach is a direct fit
- Could create girls-specific cohort
- Emphasize Zone framework (identity barriers)
- Provide belief-change metrics

## Interview #5: Mr. Otieno (The Overwhelmed Pragmatist)

**Profile:** 45 years old, Principal at mid-tier private school (350 students). Enrollment down 12%, struggling to compete, burned by previous vendors.

### Full Transcript

**Victor:** Mr. Otieno, how's enrollment this year?

**Mr. Otieno:** *grimaces* Down 12%. Parents are choosing other schools.

**Victor:** Why?

**Mr. Otieno:** They say we're "not keeping up." Other schools have smart boards, coding classes, robotics. We have... chalkboards and textbooks.

**Victor:** What are you doing about it?

**Mr. Otieno:** Trying everything. I added a computer lab last year—cost 400,000 shillings. But the computers just sit there. No one knows what to do with them.

**Victor:** No one knows what to do with them?

**Mr. Otieno:** Our teachers weren't trained in tech. They teach from textbooks. The computers are for "computer studies" class—typing, Microsoft Office. Parents want more, but I don't know how to deliver it.

**Victor:** Have companies offered you programs?

**Mr. Otieno:** *laughs bitterly* Every week. Everyone wants to sell me something. Coding programs, robotics kits, AI courses. They all promise to "transform education."

**Victor:** Why haven't you bought any?

**Mr. Otieno:** Because I've been burned. Three years ago, I paid 150,000 shillings for a "STEM program." The company came twice, then disappeared. Equipment is still in storage. I can't afford to waste money like that again.

**Victor:** If someone approached you about an AI program, what would you need to say yes?

**Mr. Otieno:** *carefully* I'd need proof it's real. Not a slideshow, not promises. Schools using it, students finishing it, parents happy with it. I'd need a trial—let me see it work before I commit. And I'd need it to be affordable. I don't have 500,000 shillings lying around.

**Victor:** If it worked, what would change for your school?

**Mr. Otieno:** I'd finally have something to tell parents. "We teach AI. Your child will be ready for university and the job market." Right now, I have nothing. And that's why we're losing students.

---

### Key Insights

**Context:**
- Enrollment down 12%
- Computer lab (400K) sits unused — no teacher training
- Burned by STEM vendor who disappeared after 2 sessions
- Desperate for "something to tell parents"

**Trust Issues:**
- Bombarded by vendor promises
- Previous 150K investment = wasted (vendor disappeared)
- Equipment still in storage
- "I can't afford to waste money like that again"

**Decision Criteria:**
- Proof first (schools using it, students finishing, parents happy)
- Trial before commitment
- Affordable (not 500K)
- Actually works (not just promises)

**Jobs to Be Done:**
1. "Give me something to stop the enrollment bleeding"
2. "Don't disappear after taking my money"
3. "Let me trial it before I commit"

**B2B Positioning:**
- Lead with proof from similar schools
- Offer pilot/trial program
- Emphasize ongoing support (we won't disappear)
- Position as enrollment differentiator

## B2B Copy Framework

### Opening Hook Options

**Competitive Schools:**
> "Three schools in your area already teach AI. What do you tell parents when they ask about yours?"

**Burned Schools:**
> "You've been promised 'transformation' before. Here's what's different about K2M: [proof]"

**Mission-Driven Schools:**
> "Most tech programs teach skills first and ignore identity. Your students say 'Tech isn't for people like me.' We address that first."

### Proof Structure

1. **Case Study:** "[School Name] ran K2M for 50 Form 4 students. Here's what happened..."
2. **Completion Rate:** "92% completion (vs. 50% typical for online courses)"
3. **Parent Satisfaction:** "Parent feedback after cohort completion"
4. **Enrollment Impact:** "[School Name] used this in admissions and saw X% increase in applications"

### Risk Reduction

1. **Pilot Program:** "Run it with 10 students first. If it doesn't work, you owe nothing."
2. **Ongoing Support:** "We don't disappear. Weekly check-ins throughout the cohort."
3. **Proof Before Payment:** "Talk to 3 principals already using K2M."

### CTA Options

- "Request a Pilot Proposal"
- "Talk to Schools Like Yours"
- "See the Curriculum"
- "Schedule a 15-Minute Call"

## Implementation Notes for B2B

### Pilot Program Structure
- 10 students per school
- 6 weeks, fully managed
- School observes entire process
- No payment required for pilot
- Convert to paid school-wide after success

### Success Metrics for Schools
- Completion rate (target: >85%)
- Student confidence change (pre/post survey)
- Parent satisfaction (post-cohort survey)
- Admissions impact (track mentions in tours)

### Support Model
- Dedicated school liaison
- Weekly progress updates to principal
- Marketing assets provided (website copy, brochure text, social media posts)
- Post-cohort report with data
