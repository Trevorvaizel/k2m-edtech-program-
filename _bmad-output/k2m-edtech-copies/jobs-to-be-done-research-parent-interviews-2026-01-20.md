## Interview #1: Mrs. Kimani (The Professional Worrier)

**Profile:** 45 years old, accountant in Nairobi, mother of Amara (CS student). Middle-class professional, actively tries to prepare daughter but feels overwhelmed by options.

### Full Transcript

**Victor:** Mrs. Kimani, your daughter starts university soon. Congratulations.

**Mrs. Kimani:** Thank you. It's exciting but also... *trails off*

**Victor:** Also what?

**Mrs. Kimani:** Terrifying. The world she's entering is not the one I knew. When I went to university, you studied, got your degree, got a job. Now? I read articles every week about AI replacing jobs, tech disrupting industries. I don't even understand half of it.

**Victor:** When was the last time you spent money on Amara's education outside of school fees?

**Mrs. Kimani:** Three months ago. I paid 15,000 shillings for a coding bootcamp. She said her friends were doing it, and she needed to keep up.

**Victor:** Did she finish it?

**Mrs. Kimani:** *sighs* No. She went to two sessions, then said the instructor was "too fast" and she felt lost. 15,000 shillings gone.

**Victor:** How did that feel?

**Mrs. Kimani:** Like I'm throwing money at a problem I don't understand. I don't know if she needs coding, or AI, or what. But everyone says she needs *something*, so I keep trying.

**Victor:** Everyone. Who's everyone?

**Mrs. Kimani:** Her teachers. My colleagues at work. Articles I see online. My brother-in-law works in IT—he keeps telling me "if Amara doesn't learn AI now, she'll struggle to find a job."

**Victor:** What did you do with that advice?

**Mrs. Kimani:** I googled "AI courses for students Kenya." Got overwhelmed. Some cost 50,000 shillings, some are free, some are online, some in-person. I don't know which one is real and which is a scam.

**Victor:** So what did you do?

**Mrs. Kimani:** Nothing. I'm still trying to figure it out. But she starts university in three weeks, and I feel like I'm failing her by not having this solved already.

**Victor:** If you could wave a magic wand and know one thing about any program you pay for, what would it be?

**Mrs. Kimani:** That it actually works. That she doesn't quit after two days like every other thing I've paid for. And that when she's done, I can see—not guess, *see*—that she's better prepared than she was before.

**Victor:** How would you see that?

**Mrs. Kimani:** She'd be using it. Like, I'd watch her do something with AI that she couldn't do before, and I'd think "okay, that money did something."

**Victor:** Would you tell anyone if it worked?

**Mrs. Kimani:** *laughs* Oh, absolutely. I'd tell every parent in my office. We're all in the same boat—trying to prepare our kids for jobs we don't understand.

---

### Key Insights

**Past Behaviors:**
- Paid 15,000 KES for coding bootcamp → daughter quit after 2 sessions
- Googled "AI courses for students Kenya" → overwhelmed, did nothing
- Relies on brother-in-law's IT advice but can't evaluate programs herself

**Emotional Triggers:**
- Terrified by changing world ("not the one I knew")
- Feeling like she's "failing" daughter
- Overwhelmed by options (free vs. paid, online vs. in-person)
- Throwing money at problems she doesn't understand

**Jobs to Be Done:**
1. "Help me stop throwing money at things she quits"
2. "Show me something I can SEE is working"
3. "Tell me which option is real vs. scam"

**Distribution Channel:**
- Would tell "every parent in my office" if something worked
- Office colleagues = primary word-of-mouth network

**Language to Use in Marketing:**
- "She won't quit this one" (addresses abandonment fear)
- "See it working" (visible proof)
- "Real vs. scam" (trust validation)

## Interview #2: Mr. Omondi (The High-Stakes Investor)

**Profile:** 52 years old, businessman from rural area, father of Joseph (first-gen student). Sold land to pay for university. Enormous expectations.

### Full Transcript

**Victor:** Mr. Omondi, you sold land to pay for Joseph's university fees. That's a serious investment.

**Mr. Omondi:** It's not just fees. It's his future. My future. Our family's future.

**Victor:** Tell me what you expect from this investment.

**Mr. Omondi:** Joseph will graduate, get a job in Nairobi—good salary, respectable company. He'll help his siblings go to university. He'll buy land, build a house. That's what this education is for.

**Victor:** What worries you most about that plan?

**Mr. Omondi:** *pause* That he won't be ready. He's a smart boy, but he's from a small town. The competition in Nairobi—those kids have been preparing since primary school. Private tutors, international schools, computers in their homes. Joseph has to catch up fast.

**Victor:** Last time you spent money to help him "catch up"—what did you buy?

**Mr. Omondi:** I bought him a laptop. 60,000 shillings. He needed it for university.

**Victor:** Is he using it?

**Mr. Omondi:** *hesitates* He uses it. But I don't know if he's learning or just... watching.

**Victor:** Did you ask him?

**Mr. Omondi:** I don't want to pressure him too much before he even starts. But yes, I worry.

**Victor:** If someone offered you a program—any kind of training for Joseph before university—what would make you say yes immediately?

**Mr. Omondi:** If it gave him what those Nairobi kids already have. If it closed the gap. I don't want him walking into that lecture hall feeling like he doesn't belong there.

**Victor:** How would you know it worked?

**Mr. Omondi:** He'd talk differently. More confident. Right now, when I ask him about university, he says "I'll try my best." I want him to say "I'm ready."

**Victor:** If it worked, would you recommend it to others?

**Mr. Omondi:** I'd tell everyone in my village. Every parent with a child going to university. I'd say "don't let them go unprepared like we used to. This is how you give them a chance."

---

### Key Insights

**Past Behaviors:**
- Sold land to pay for university
- Bought 60,000 KES laptop — unsure if son is learning or just watching YouTube
- Doesn't pressure son directly but worries constantly

**Emotional Triggers:**
- Intergenerational pressure (siblings' future depends on Joseph)
- Rural/urban status gap ("Nairobi kids")
- Fear of son "not belonging"
- Wants to hear "I'm ready" instead of "I'll try my best"

**Jobs to Be Done:**
1. "Close the gap between my son and Nairobi kids"
2. "Make him feel like he belongs, not like he's behind"
3. "Give me confidence my sacrifice will pay off"

**Distribution Channel:**
- Would tell "everyone in my village"
- Village network = high-trust word-of-mouth

**Language to Use in Marketing:**
- "What Nairobi kids already have" (his exact phrase)
- "Walk in ready, not catching up"
- "Belongs there" (identity/status)

## Interview #3: Mrs. Wambui (The Skeptical Pragmatist)

**Profile:** 48 years old, single mother, teacher, mother of Wanjiru (Media student). Tight budget, highly skeptical of program promises due to past disappointments.

### Full Transcript

**Victor:** Mrs. Wambui, your daughter is studying media. How do you feel about that?

**Mrs. Wambui:** *laughs* Honestly? Nervous. Media doesn't pay like medicine or engineering. But she's talented, and I want her to follow her passion. Within reason.

**Victor:** Within reason. What does that mean?

**Mrs. Wambui:** It means she needs to be smart about it. The creative industries are hard. Lots of competition, not many jobs. So she needs every advantage she can get.

**Victor:** Tell me about the last time you paid for something to give her an advantage.

**Mrs. Wambui:** Last year, I paid for her to attend a film workshop. 5,000 shillings for a weekend. She loved it, learned video editing, made a short film. That felt worth it.

**Victor:** What made it worth it?

**Mrs. Wambui:** I could see the result. She showed me the film, I watched it, I thought "this is real." It wasn't just theory—she made something.

**Victor:** Have you thought about preparing her for university?

**Mrs. Wambui:** I think about it every day. But I'm a teacher—my salary is tight. I can't afford expensive programs. And honestly, I don't trust most of them.

**Victor:** You don't trust them. Why not?

**Mrs. Wambui:** Because they promise everything and deliver nothing. I've seen it. "Guaranteed job after completion"—but the job never comes. "Industry-recognized certificate"—but employers don't care. It's marketing.

**Victor:** So what would make you trust something enough to pay for it?

**Mrs. Wambui:** *thinks carefully* If other parents I know tried it first and said it worked. If the price was fair—not cheap, but not exploitative. And if I could see, clearly, what my daughter would have at the end that she doesn't have now.

**Victor:** What would she have at the end?

**Mrs. Wambui:** A skill she can use immediately. Not in five years. Now. Something she can show employers or clients and say "look what I can do."

**Victor:** If it worked, would you tell other parents?

**Mrs. Wambui:** If it actually worked? I'd tell every parent in my school. Teachers talk, and we all want what's best for our kids. But we're also skeptical—we've been burned before.

---

### Key Insights

**Past Behaviors:**
- Paid 5,000 KES for film workshop → worth it because visible output (short film)
- Teacher salary = tight budget, can't afford waste
- Trust scarred by programs that over-promised

**Emotional Triggers:**
- Worry about daughter's career path (media = risky)
- Skepticism from past disappointments
- Need for tangible, immediate results

**Jobs to Be Done:**
1. "Show me what she'll have at the end that she doesn't have now"
2. "Don't waste my limited money on empty promises"
3. "Give her something she can use immediately, not eventually"

**Distribution Channel:**
- Would tell "every parent in my school"
- Teacher network = high-credibility word-of-mouth

**Language to Use in Marketing:**
- "Something she can show" (tangible proof)
- "Use immediately" (not theoretical)
- "Other parents tried it and said it worked" (social proof)

## Interview #4: Dr. Mutiso (The Tech-Literate Investor)

**Profile:** 50 years old, works in tech sector, father of Brian (SWE student). Affluent, can evaluate program quality, has high standards.

### Full Transcript

**Victor:** Dr. Mutiso, your son is already technical. What's left for you to worry about?

**Dr. Mutiso:** *laughs* Everything. Just because Brian can code doesn't mean he knows how to navigate the industry. There's a gap between "can write code" and "can get hired."

**Victor:** Tell me about that gap.

**Dr. Mutiso:** Soft skills. Communication. Understanding business problems, not just technical ones. And now, with AI—knowing how to use it without letting it replace your thinking.

**Victor:** When was the last time you tried to help Brian with that?

**Dr. Mutiso:** I sent him links to articles about AI in software development. Told him "you need to stay ahead of this." He said "okay, Dad," and I don't know if he read them.

**Victor:** Did you follow up?

**Dr. Mutiso:** *ruefully* No. He's 19. If I push too hard, he shuts down. But I worry he's not taking it seriously enough.

**Victor:** If someone offered a program for Brian—something to prepare him for the AI-driven job market—what would make you interested?

**Dr. Mutiso:** It would have to be rigorous. Not fluff. I'd want to see the curriculum, the outcomes, the methodology. I work in tech—I can tell when something is real and when it's just buzzwords.

**Victor:** What would "real" look like?

**Dr. Mutiso:** Clear learning objectives. Practical application. And ideally, some kind of peer learning—Brian learns better when he's collaborating with others who challenge him.

**Victor:** Would price matter?

**Dr. Mutiso:** Not as much as quality. I'll pay for something good. But I won't pay for something mediocre just because it's expensive. There's a difference between premium and overpriced.

**Victor:** If it worked—if Brian came out of it more prepared—what would you do?

**Dr. Mutiso:** I'd recommend it to my colleagues. We have a WhatsApp group—about 40 tech professionals, all with kids. We share resources all the time. If something works, it spreads fast in that group.

---

### Key Insights

**Past Behaviors:**
- Sends articles to son, unsure if he reads them
- Can evaluate curriculum quality (tech background)
- Doesn't push too hard to avoid son shutting down

**Emotional Triggers:**
- Gap between "can code" and "can get hired"
- AI as both opportunity and threat
- Values quality over price

**Jobs to Be Done:**
1. "Give him something rigorous, not fluff"
2. "Peer learning that challenges him"
3. "Quality over price—but actual quality, not expensive mediocrity"

**Distribution Channel:**
- WhatsApp group of 40 tech professionals with kids
- High-value, high-trust network

**Language to Use in Marketing:**
- "Rigorous" and "methodology"
- "Peer learning" and "collaboration"
- "Clear learning objectives" (speaks to evaluators)

## Interview #5: Mrs. Njeri (The Anxious Believer)

**Profile:** 46 years old, small business owner, mother of Faith (Actuarial Science student). Paying 800K/year for Strathmore. Believes in transformative education.

### Full Transcript

**Victor:** Mrs. Njeri, Strathmore is expensive. That's a big commitment.

**Mrs. Njeri:** *nods* 800,000 shillings per year. It's almost everything we have. But Faith worked so hard—she deserves the best school.

**Victor:** What are you most afraid of?

**Mrs. Njeri:** That she'll get there and struggle. That we'll pay all this money and she'll come back in four years without a job. Or worse, with a degree but no confidence.

**Victor:** Tell me about a time you spent money on Faith's education and it paid off.

**Mrs. Njeri:** Two years ago, I paid for her to join a mentorship program. 10,000 shillings for six months. She was matched with a woman who works at KCB Bank. That woman changed everything for Faith—taught her how to present herself, how to network, how to think about her career.

**Victor:** What made that worth 10,000 shillings?

**Mrs. Njeri:** I saw Faith change. She started speaking with more confidence. She made plans. She set goals. That program didn't teach her math or science—it taught her how to be the kind of person who succeeds.

**Victor:** Have you thought about preparing her for university?

**Mrs. Njeri:** Every single day. I lie awake thinking about it. She's going to be surrounded by wealthy kids who've had every opportunity. I want her to walk in there feeling like she belongs.

**Victor:** If someone offered a program to prepare her—something before university starts—what would you need to know before saying yes?

**Mrs. Njeri:** *immediate* Will it actually help her, or is it just another thing I pay for that makes me feel better but doesn't change anything?

**Victor:** How would you know it actually helped?

**Mrs. Njeri:** She'd tell me. Not because I asked, but because she'd be excited. She'd say "Mom, I learned this thing, and now I can do this." And I'd see her using it—not just talking about it, using it.

**Victor:** If it worked, what would you do?

**Mrs. Njeri:** *lights up* I'd tell every mother in my church group. We meet every Wednesday, about 30 of us. Half have children going to university this year or next. We share everything—which schools are good, which tutors work, what's worth the money. If something actually works, we all want to know.

---

### Key Insights

**Past Behaviors:**
- Paying 800K/year for Strathmore ("almost everything we have")
- Previous mentorship program (10K, 6 months) → transformative
- Lies awake thinking about daughter's readiness

**Emotional Triggers:**
- Fear of degree without job/confidence
- Wants identity transformation, not just skills
- "Belongs" with wealthy kids

**Jobs to Be Done:**
1. "Change who she IS, not just what she knows"
2. "Make her feel like she belongs with wealthy kids"
3. "Give me something real, not something that makes me feel better temporarily"

**Distribution Channel:**
- Church group of 30 mothers meeting Wednesdays
- Half have kids going to university this year
- Highest-trust, highest-conversion channel

**Language to Use in Marketing:**
- "The kind of person who succeeds" (identity transformation)
- "Walk in feeling like she belongs"
- "Actually help her" vs. "makes me feel better"

## Parent Copy Framework

### Opening Hook Options
1. Fear: "You lie awake wondering if they're ready..."
2. Social: "Other parents in your network are already doing this..."
3. Investment: "You've already invested everything..."
4. Empathy: "You don't fully understand AI yourself—how can you prepare them?"

### Proof Structure
1. Other parents' testimonials (named, specific)
2. Completion rate (vs. typical course abandonment)
3. Visible outcome description ("Watch your child demonstrate...")
4. Network endorsement ("Parents in [area] recommend this")

### CTA Framework
- Primary: Action-oriented ("Prepare My Child")
- Secondary: Information-seeking ("See If This Is Right for My Child")
- Urgency: Limited ("200 Spots for February")
