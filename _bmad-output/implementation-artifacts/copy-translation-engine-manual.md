## Executive Summary

This manual solves the **abstraction leakage problem**: how to translate deep strategic insights (JTBD research) into conversion copy that lands without losing soul or breaking attention.

Most companies have this broken pipeline:

```
Research (JTBD) → Marketing Team → "Transform your anxiety journey!" → Market ❌
```

**The problem:** Marketing announces insights instead of embodying them. This creates "slop" - copy that feels divorced from the philosophy it represents.

**Our solution:**

```
Research (JTBD) → [7-MODULE TRANSLATION ENGINE] → Copy → Market ✅
                     ↑
      (Invisible Writing Framework)
```

This engine is **repeatable, scalable, and philosophy-preserving**. Anyone trained in the M1-M7 system can translate strategic truth into conversion copy without dilution.

## Part 1: System Architecture

### 1.1 The Translation Problem

**What Goes Wrong:**

You do rigorous research (Jobs-to-be-Done interviews, synthesis, validation). You discover genuine emotional territories:
- Anxiety: "Stop feeling confused when using AI"
- Belonging: "I'm not alone in this struggle"
- Identity transformation: "I become the kind of person who adapts"

Then the copy becomes:
> "Navigate your AI literacy journey with future-proof skills!"

**Why this fails:**
1. **Abstraction leakage** - Internal model becomes public too early
2. **Announcement vs embodiment** - Tells instead of shows
3. **Attention-breaking** - Asks people to learn a new metaphor before feeling relief
4. **Cognitive overload** - Too abstract for first contact

## Part 2: The 7-Module Translation Engine

### 2.1 Module Overview

Each module fulfills a psychological function. All seven must appear for the engine to work (in long-form). For social media, 2-3 modules create complete micro-loops.

| Module | Function | Key Move | When to Use |
|--------|----------|----------|-------------|
| **M1: Destabilize** | Disorient | Break default frame before defense | Opening hooks, pattern interrupts |
| **M2: Expose** | Name autopilot | Make invisible script visible | Recognition, "that's me" moments |
| **M3: Pressure** | Make staying costly | Show inevitable cost (not threat) | Discomfort with status quo |
| **M4: Reframe** | Offer higher lens | Dissolve old question | Perspective shifts |
| **M5: Identity** | Touch self-concept | Personal implication | "I want to be that" |
| **M6: Agency** | Return responsibility | Hand back without rescuing | Decision points |
| **M7: Exit** | Deny closure | Leave tension active | Endings (no tidy conclusions) |

### 2.2 Module-by-Module Breakdown

#### Module 1: DESTABILIZE (Disorient)

**Function:** Interrupt the reader's autopilot. Break their default frame before they can defend it.

**What it does:**
- Creates a cognitive gap
- Makes the familiar feel strange
- Prevents coasting on existing assumptions

**Execution tactics:**
- Open with contradiction: "Most people think X, but actually..."
- Present familiar situation from unfamiliar angle
- Use precise specificity: "85% of AI implementations fail"
- Ask question revealing hidden assumptions

**Example:**
> "85% of AI implementations fail. Not because of bad technology. Because of unprepared humans."

**Quality check:**
- [ ] Does it interrupt a default frame?
- [ ] Is it specific enough to signal depth?
- [ ] Would someone stop scrolling?

**Warning:**
- Too weak = feels like ordinary content
- Too aggressive = readers defend rather than absorb

---

#### Module 2: EXPOSE (Name the Autopilot)

**Function:** Make visible the invisible system operating on the reader.

**What it does:**
- Names the cultural script, mental model, or framework running in background
- Shows reader they are inside a pattern, not above it
- Creates "I've been doing this without knowing it" recognition

**Execution tactics:**
- Describe pattern without judgment: "Most organizations do X because Y"
- Use systems language to depersonalize: "The default playbook assumes..."
- Show logic making current behavior seem rational
- Reveal hidden incentives or conditioning

**Example:**
> "You've been taught to ask 'which tools?' before asking 'which humans?' That's the gap. The technology isn't the problem. The consciousness is."

**Quality check:**
- [ ] Does it name an invisible script?
- [ ] Is tone observational, not accusatory?
- [ ] Will they think "I've been doing this"?

**Key nuance:**
No moral superiority. You are mapping, not accusing. Reader should feel seen, not attacked.

---

#### Module 3: APPLY PRESSURE (Make Staying Costly)

**Function:** Create felt cost of remaining in current frame without using fear or urgency.

**What it does:**
- Makes status quo uncomfortable to maintain
- Shows what continuing costs (not what might happen, what IS happening)
- Creates internal tension without external threat

**Execution tactics:**
- Present cumulative costs compounding over time
- Show what current approach makes impossible
- Use inevitability language: "This isn't a risk—it's already occurring"
- Make reader feel weight of participation

**Example:**
> "Every month you delay, you're training your team to resist what's coming. By doing nothing, you don't stay the same. You fall behind."

**Quality check:**
- [ ] Is cost stated as inevitable, not threatening?
- [ ] Does it feel like diagnosis, not warning?
- [ ] Is it free of melodrama or catastrophizing?

**Critical balance:**
Too dramatic = lose trust. Too vague = lose force. Cost should feel inevitable, not threatening.

---

#### Module 4: REFRAME (Offer a Higher Lens)

**Function:** Provide new perspective that dissolves the original question rather than answering it.

**What it does:**
- Shifts level of analysis
- Makes previous frame appear as one option among many
- Creates "the question I was asking was wrong" realization

**Execution tactics:**
- Introduce framework containing old frame as subset
- Use developmental or systems language
- Show how new lens explains anomalies old lens couldn't
- Present reframe as discovered, not invented

**Example:**
> "The question isn't 'which AI tools?' The question is 'which humans are ready?'

That changes everything about where you start."

**Quality check:**
- [ ] Does old question dissolve (not just get answered)?
- [ ] Is new lens visibly higher-order?
- [ ] Can they not go back to old frame?

**Why this works:**
The old question dissolves. Reader cannot go back to treating it as primary.

---

#### Module 5: TOUCH IDENTITY (Make It Personal)

**Function:** Connect abstract pattern to reader's self-concept, role, or lived experience.

**What it does:**
- Moves from "this is interesting" to "this describes how I operate"
- Implicates reader without accusing
- Makes piece feel written for them specifically

**Execution tactics:**
- Use "people like you" language carefully
- Reference reader's likely role, decisions, or context
- Show how they are participating in pattern (not just observing)
- Ask questions forcing self-reflection

**Example:**
> "You've probably felt this gap yourself—between the strategy decks and the actual adoption. That gap lives in your leadership team's consciousness. And it starts with you."

**Quality check:**
- [ ] Does it describe who they want to be?
- [ ] Is there implicit pressure (are you one of the few)?
- [ ] Does current behavior conflict with aspired identity?

**Timing matters:**
Too early = defensiveness. Too late = feels disconnected. Identity pressure should arrive after reader has new lens to hold it.

---

#### Module 6: RETURN AGENCY (Hand It Back)

**Function:** Transfer responsibility to reader without rescuing them with solutions.

**What it does:**
- Puts choice back in reader's hands
- Frames next moves as their decision, not your prescription
- Respects capacity to act without step-by-step instructions

**Execution tactics:**
- Use "you get to decide" language
- Present possibilities, not mandates
- Acknowledge difficulty without offering escape
- Frame inaction as choice with consequences

**Example:**
> "You can address this now—starting with your own development. Or wait until failure forces the conversation.

Either way, the question arrives. You're just choosing the cost."

**Quality check:**
- [ ] Is responsibility clearly with reader?
- [ ] Is there NO rescue (no step-by-step system)?
- [ ] Does weight stay with them after reading?

**Key nuance:**
No rescuing. Don't provide action plans that let them externalize responsibility. Weight stays with them.

---

#### Module 7: UNRESOLVED EXIT (Deny Closure)

**Function:** End without tidy resolution. Leave tension active so piece echoes after reading.

**What it does:**
- Prevents filing insight away as "done"
- Keeps question alive in mind
- Creates after-effect (think about it hours or days later)

**Execution tactics:**
- End with question having no easy answer
- Leave one tension unresolved
- Resist urge to summarize or conclude neatly
- Let final line point toward something larger

**Example:**
> "The winners won't have the best tools. They'll have the most conscious humans.

What does that require of you?"

**Quality check:**
- [ ] Is there NO tidy conclusion?
- [ ] Will they keep thinking later?
- [ ] Does it point toward action without specifying?

**Warning:**
Closure anywhere near end kills after-effect. Reader should leave with something to keep processing.

### 2.3 Module Combinations (Social Media Patterns)

For social media, use 2-3 modules per post. Each post must be complete enough to stand alone, yet incomplete enough to create hunger.

**Type A: Single Post (Text Only)**
```
[Hook - M1 or M5] 1 sentence
[Body - M2 or M4] 2-4 sentences
[Exit - M7 or M6] 1 sentence (no closure)
```

**Proven combinations:**
- **COMBO A:** M1 + M4 + M7 (Hook challenges → Reframe offered → Loop stays open)
- **COMBO B:** M2 + M3 + M6 (Script named → Cost shown → Agency returned)
- **COMBO C:** M5 + M2 + M7 (Identity challenged → Script exposed → No closure)

**Type B: Carousel (Multi-Slide)**
Full 7-module sequence distributed across slides:

```
SLIDE 1: HOOK (M1)         5-15 words
SLIDE 2: EXPOSE (M2)       30-60 words
SLIDE 3: PRESSURE (M3)     30-50 words
SLIDE 4: REFRAME (M4)      40-80 words
SLIDE 5: PATH (M4/M2)      50-80 words
SLIDE 6: IDENTITY (M5+M6)  40-70 words
SLIDE 7: EXIT (M7)         15-30 words
```

**Type C: Image-Text Hybrid**
```
IMAGE: Visual metaphor (solitary figure, path, light/dark)
TEXT OVERLAY: [M1] or [M5] - Maximum 15 words
CAPTION: [M2] + [M6] - Expand and transfer agency
```

---

### 2.4 Juggling Patterns (Long-Form Ordering)

Modules can be reordered for different psychological effects.

**Pattern 1: Soft Infiltration**
Order: 4 → 2 → 1 → 3 → 5 → 6 → 7

Feels like: Quiet, almost gentle. Reader only realizes later something changed.

Best for: Thoughtful audiences, philosophical topics, readers allergic to confrontation.

**Pattern 2: Identity First Strike**
Order: 1 → 5 → 2 → 3 → 4 → 6 → 7

Feels like: Personal, uncomfortable, hard to disengage.

Best for: Personal development, individual agency narratives, readers open to self-examination.

Risk: Overuse creates defensiveness.

**Pattern 3: Systemic Trap**
Order: 2 → 1 → 3 → 4 → 5 → 6 → 7

Feels like: Cold, inevitable, difficult to argue with.

Best for: Systems thinkers, policy topics, ethically serious audiences.

**Pattern 4: Late Identity Reveal**
Order: 1 → 2 → 3 → 4 → 5 → 6 → 7 (identity pressure delayed)

Feels like: Safe... until it isn't.

Best for: Essays, readers valuing objectivity, building case before making personal.

**Pattern 5: Agency Weight Close**
Order: Standard sequence with strong M6, extended M7

Feels like: Heavy, quiet, lingering.

Best for: Mature audiences, ethical/existential topics, pieces where responsibility is the point.

## Part 3: The Abstraction Ladder (4-Level Translation)

### 3.1 Ladder Overview

**The Problem We Solve:**

Internal language (philosophy, frameworks, models) ≠ External language (what users hear)

If you make internal language public too early:
- "This sounds academic"
- "This feels theoretical"
- "I don't know if this is for me"

That's an attention mismatch, not a concept problem.

**The Solution: Separate INTERNAL architecture from EXTERNAL language**

```
Layer 1 (what clients hear): Concrete. Immediate. Familiar.
Layer 2 (what they gradually discover): Patterns. Framing. Structure.
Layer 3 (what you and facilitators use): Territory, perception zones, cartography.
```

### 3.2 The Four Levels

**Level 3: Internal Philosophy (Facilitators Only)**

What it is:
- Territory, zones, cartography models
- Perception shifts, cognitive architecture
- "Zone 0 → Zone 4 journey"

Who sees it:
- Facilitators
- Advanced participants
- You (Trevor)

Example:
> "We guide learners through Zone 0 (unconscious confusion) to Zone 4 (conscious agency) using perception shift exercises."

---

**Level 2: Strategic Truth (Design Guidance)**

What it is:
- JTBD jobs mapped to emotional territories
- User needs, not features
- Design principles

Who sees it:
- Product team
- Content designers
- Facilitator training

Example:
> "Students need anxiety relief ('stop feeling confused') and belonging ('I'm not alone') before identity transformation ('I become the kind of person who adapts')."

---

**Level 1: External Messaging (User Sees)**

What it is:
- 7-module execution
- Concrete, immediate, familiar
- No territory language

Example:
> "Sometimes AI works. Sometimes it doesn't. And you don't know why.

The habits you form now will follow you into university.

Small cohort. Real practice. Week 1 you'll feel the shift."

---

**Level 0: User Ear (What They Feel)**

What it is:
- Recognition, not announcement
- "They get me"
- "I can't unsee what they pointed at"

Example reaction:
> "Oh... they understand what I'm worried about."

### 3.3 Translation Workflow

**STEP 1: Start with JTBD Truth (Level 2)**
Input: "Students fear falling behind as AI becomes more embedded"

**STEP 2: Apply 7-Module Engine**
- M1: "The habits you form now will follow you"
- M3: "By doing nothing, you don't stay the same. You fall behind."
- M4: "The question isn't 'which AI tools?'"
- M7: "What does that require of you?"

**STEP 3: Descend Ladder (Level 1)**
> "The habits you form now will follow you into university.
> By doing nothing, you don't stay the same. You fall behind.
> The question isn't 'which AI tools?' The question is 'how do you think?'"

**STEP 4: User Ear Reaction (Level 0)**
"They understand what I'm worried about. This is for me."

### 3.4 Critical Insight

**The learner does not need to know they're on a map for the map to work.**

You still:
- Design exercises as boundary crossings
- Structure Discord by zones
- Move people through perception shifts
- Track language changes
- Scaffold agency

You just don't say that upfront.

They experience it first.

Later, some will say: "Oh... this is like stages."

Only then do you optionally introduce the map.

## Part 4: The Extraction & Repurposing Workflow

### 4.1 From Long-Form to Social Media

**The Fundamental Principle:**

- **Long-form = One deep cut** (all 7 modules in sequence, 1,500-3,000 words)
- **Social media = Death by a thousand paper cuts** (2-3 modules per post, 50-300 words)

Each post must be complete enough to stand alone, yet incomplete enough to create hunger for more.

### 4.2 Step-by-Step Extraction Process

**STEP 1: Read & Tag (15 minutes)**

Read your long-form piece and highlight/tag every section by module:
```
[M1] = Destabilize (frame breaks, contradictions, pattern interrupts)
[M2] = Expose (default logic named, invisible scripts surfaced)
[M3] = Pressure (costs shown without drama)
[M4] = Reframe (higher lens offered, question dissolved)
[M5] = Identity (self-concept challenged or affirmed)
[M6] = Agency (responsibility transferred, no rescue)
[M7] = Exit (tension left unresolved)
```

Also highlight **"payload sentences"** - lines carrying maximum psychological weight per word:
- Paradoxes: "If you need to memorize it, you don't understand it"
- Reversals: "The best way to learn skills is to stop trying to learn skills"
- Identity statements: "Very few people have the courage"
- Specificity: "85% of AI implementations fail"
- Metaphors: "Your mind is the digestive system for reality"

---

**STEP 2: Extract Hooks (10 minutes)**

Pull all [M1] fragments and refine into 5-15 word hooks.

**Extraction Formula:**
```
LONG-FORM: "Most organizations focus on AI tools first, then wonder
why adoption fails. The conventional implementation playbook has it
backwards—85% of AI projects fail because we're asking the wrong
question entirely."

EXTRACTED HOOK: "85% of AI implementations fail.
Not because of bad technology.
Because of unprepared humans."
```

**Quality Check:**
- [ ] Does it contradict something reader assumes?
- [ ] Is it specific enough to signal depth?
- [ ] Can they NOT scroll past without reacting?

---

**STEP 3: Extract Bodies (15 minutes)**

Pull [M2], [M3], [M4] fragments and pair with hooks for single posts.

**Extraction Formula:**
```
LONG-FORM: "Leaders who don't understand how to think strategically
with AI, teams who haven't developed the cognitive capabilities to
work alongside intelligent systems, organizations that try to bolt
AI onto unchanged human processes—this is the consciousness gap in
AI implementation."

EXTRACTED BODY: "You've been taught to ask 'which tools?' before
asking 'which humans?' That's the gap. The technology isn't the
problem. The consciousness is."
```

**Quality Check:**
- [ ] Does it name something they do unconsciously?
- [ ] Is tone observational, not accusatory?
- [ ] Will they think "I've been doing this"?

---

**STEP 4: Extract Identity/Agency (10 minutes)**

Pull [M5] and [M6] fragments. These often become carousel slides 5-6 or pair with strong hooks for identity-forward posts.

**Extraction Formula:**
```
LONG-FORM: "The organizations that will win the AI age won't be the
ones with the best tools. They'll be the ones with humans conscious
enough to use them. Very few executives have the patience to invest
in human development before showing AI ROI."

EXTRACTED IDENTITY: "The winners won't have the best tools.
They'll have the most conscious humans.

Very few leaders are willing to invest in human development first.
Are you one of them?"
```

**Quality Check:**
- [ ] Does it describe who they want to be?
- [ ] Is there implicit pressure?
- [ ] Does current behavior conflict with aspired identity?

---

**STEP 5: Design Carousel (20 minutes)**

Select strongest hook and map full 7-module sequence across 5-7 slides.

**Carousel Blueprint (7 slides):**

```
SLIDE 1: HOOK (M1)
├── Pattern interrupt
├── Contradiction or provocative claim
├── Visual: Bold text, minimal design
└── Goal: Stop the scroll

SLIDE 2: EXPOSE (M2)
├── Name the invisible script
├── "Most people think..." / "You've been told..."
├── Visual: Text only, clean
└── Goal: Recognition ("that's me")

SLIDE 3: PRESSURE (M3)
├── Show the cost (no drama)
├── "By doing nothing, you don't stay the same..."
├── Visual: Text only or metaphor image
└── Goal: Discomfort with status quo

SLIDE 4: REFRAME (M4)
├── Offer higher lens
├── "The question isn't X, it's Y"
├── Visual: Text only, emphasis on key phrase
└── Goal: Old question dissolves

SLIDE 5: IDENTITY (M5)
├── Challenge or affirm self-concept
├── "Very few people..." / "This is what it means to..."
├── Visual: Text or aspirational figure image
└── Goal: "I want to be that person"

SLIDE 6: AGENCY (M6)
├── Hand back responsibility
├── One clear decision point (not a system)
├── Visual: Text only
└── Goal: Weight stays with reader

SLIDE 7: EXIT (M7)
├── Question without answer OR
├── Statement that points beyond
├── NO summary, NO CTA to close loop
└── Goal: They keep thinking after swipe
```

---

**STEP 6: Create Image Post (10 minutes)**

Identify core metaphor in your piece. Find or create visual embodying it. Write 15-word overlay + 50-word caption.

**Visual Language:**
- Black and white illustrations
- Single figure in contemplative pose
- Vast space (void, sky, path ahead)
- Light source the figure faces or sits in

**Psychological Function:**
- Reader projects themselves into solitary figure
- Journey/path imagery encodes "decision point"
- Light/dark contrast = current state vs. possible state

### 4.3 Output Multiplication

From **one long-form piece (1,500-3,000 words)** extract:

```
1x Long-form essay
    ↓
├── 3-5 Single Posts (Type A)
├── 1-2 Carousels (Type B)
└── 1 Image Post (Type C)
```

**This is content multiplication without quality loss.**

## Part 5: Output Templates & Patterns

### 5.1 Message Frameworks

**Framework 1: Mirror**
Modules: M2 + M5
Function: "You're not alone"

Example:
> "Most people feel confused when AI works sometimes and fails other times.

That's not a user error. That's a design gap—and you're not alone in it."

---

**Framework 2: Myth**
Modules: M1 + M4
Function: "Prompts aren't the skill"

Example:
> "Copy prompts all you want. You're still not learning how to steer.

The skill isn't prompt engineering. It's learning how to think before you interact."

---

**Framework 3: Before/After**
Modules: M2 + M4
Function: "Guessing → Clarity"

Example:
> "Before: guessing what prompts will work.
After: understanding why AI responds the way it does.

The difference isn't more practice. It's better questions."

---

**Framework 4: Invitation**
Modules: M5 + M6 + M7
Function: "Small cohort, real practice"

Example:
> "Very few people take the time to understand how AI actually works.

Small cohort. Real practice. Week 1 you'll feel the shift.

What would it look like if you stopped guessing?"

---

**Framework 5: Proof**
Modules: M3 + M5
Function: "Here's what changes in 7 days"

Example:
> "The habits you form in week 1 will either compound or decay.

By day 7, you'll stop copying prompts and start understanding what makes AI work.

Or you'll keep guessing. The choice is yours."

## Part 6: QA Protocol (Validation System)

### 6.1 Engine Test Metrics (Before Publishing)

Run this checklist for EVERY piece of copy:

**Module Completion:**
- [ ] **M1 Present**: Does opening interrupt default frame?
- [ ] **M2 Present**: Is invisible script named (no accusation)?
- [ ] **M3 Present**: Is cost inevitable not threatening?
- [ ] **M4 Present**: Does old question dissolve?
- [ ] **M5 Present**: Is identity touched at right moment (not too early, not too late)?
- [ ] **M6 Present**: Is agency returned (no rescue)?
- [ ] **M7 Present**: Is exit open (no closure)?

*Note: For social media, check only the modules you're using (2-3 modules per post)*

---

**Philosophy Alignment:**
- [ ] **JTBD Truth Preserved**: Does copy reflect strategic research finding?
- [ ] **Invisible Writing Respected**: No abstraction leakage?
- [ ] **Abstraction Appropriate**: Right level for target audience?
- [ ] **No Moral Superiority**: Mapping, not judging?
- [ ] **No Emotional Bullying**: Inevitability, not threat?
- [ ] **No Rescuing**: Responsibility stays with reader?

---

**Attention Design (Caravaggio's 5 Gears):**
- [ ] **Friction Removed**: Can they enter easily?
- [ ] **Entry Designed**: Is there a tiny first step?
- [ ] **Cognitive Load Managed**: 3-second rule passed?
- [ ] **Hierarchy Intentional**: Eye journey designed?
- [ ] **Timeline Orchestrated**: Urgency without panic?

---

### 6.2 User Validation Protocol

**The Litmus Test:**

After reading your piece, if someone says:

> ✅ "I don't agree, but I can't unsee what you pointed at."

**You executed the mode correctly.**

If they say:

> ❌ "That was interesting" (and moves on unchanged)

**One or more modules failed.**

---

**Testing Protocol:**

1. **Test with 3-5 real users**
   - Show them the copy
   - Ask: "What does this make you feel?"
   - Ask: "What would you do next?"

2. **Measure comprehension**
   - Can they paraphrase the core message in their own words?
   - Do they feel it was written for them specifically?

3. **Track conversion**
   - Click-through rate
   - Sign-up rate
   - "Not ready" feedback (why?)

---

### 6.3 Common Pitfalls & Rescue Protocols

**Pitfall 1: M1 Too Weak**
Symptom: "Feels like ordinary content"
Rescue: Strengthen hook with contradiction or specificity

**Pitfall 2: M1 Too Aggressive**
Symptom: Readers defend, don't absorb
Rescue: Soften with "Most people think..." framing

**Pitfall 3: M2 With Judgment**
Symptom: Readers feel accused
Rescue: Use systems language ("The default playbook...")

**Pitfall 4: M3 With Fear**
Symptom: Feels like threat, not diagnosis
Rescue: Change to inevitability language ("This is already occurring")

**Pitfall 5: M6 With Rescue**
Symptom: Readers externalize responsibility
Rescue: Remove step-by-step solutions, frame as decision

**Pitfall 6: M7 With Closure**
Symptom: No after-effect
Rescue: Remove summary, end with question or forward-pointing statement

### 7.2 When to Use Which Abstraction Level

**Use Level 3 (Internal Philosophy) with:**
- Facilitators
- Advanced participants (Week 4+)
- Training materials

**Use Level 2 (Strategic Truth) with:**
- Product team
- Content designers
- Partner organizations

**Use Level 1 (External Messaging) with:**
- Marketing copy (all channels)
- Landing pages
- Outreach campaigns

**Never use Level 3 language in Level 1 contexts.**

This is the #1 cause of abstraction leakage.
