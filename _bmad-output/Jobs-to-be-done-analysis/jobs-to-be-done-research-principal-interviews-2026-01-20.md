# Jobs-to-be-Done Research: Principal Interviews
**Date:** January 20, 2026
**Researchers:** Victor (Innovation Strategist) + John (Product Manager) + Maya (Design Thinking Coach)
**Methodology:** The Mom Test Framework (Rob Fitzpatrick)
**Sample:** 5 Kenyan school principals (ages 41-58)

---

## Executive Summary

Interviews with five Kenyan school principals revealed that **schools are not monolithic** — different school types have dramatically different Jobs-to-be-Done when recommending external programs to students.

**Key Finding:** The current K2M strategy (mass-market cohort, 3,500 KES) is a **good fit for only 20% of schools** (private mid-tier schools like Mrs. Kamau's). The remaining 80% require different models, pricing, and value propositions.

**Critical Insight:** Schools make recommendations based on **risk mitigation first, student outcomes second**. "Proven elsewhere" and "who will parents blame if something goes wrong?" are the dominant decision criteria.

---

## The Mom Test Framework Applied (Principals)

**Principles Followed:**
1. ✅ Never mentioned K2M or AI cohort program
2. ✅ Asked about PAST external program recommendations, not hypothetical
3. ✅ Dug for specific decision-making processes and criteria
4. ✅ Explored risk perceptions and failure stories
5. ✅ Avoided leading questions about "importance of innovation"

**Sample Diversity:**
- School types: Public large, private mid-tier, elite national, rural low-income, technical/vocational
- Student populations: 400 to 2,000 students
- Income levels: Very low to very high
- Geographic: Nairobi, rural areas
- Budget constraints: Extreme to well-funded

## Interview #1: Mr. Omondi (Age 52, Nairobi Public Secondary)

### Persona Profile
- Principal of large public secondary school (1,200 students)
- 28 years in education
- Mixed income, primarily middle/working class families
- **Core pressures:** Board wants exam results; parents want guidance; limited budget
- **Decision mindset:** Risk-averse "smart follower"

### Key Quotes

**On AI context:**
> "AI came up at our last board meeting. One parent asked, 'What is the school doing about AI?' I didn't have a good answer. The truth is, we're not doing anything. We don't have the budget. We don't have trained teachers."

> "Parents are confused. They want guidance from us, but we don't know what to tell them."

**On decision criteria:**
> "The provider came to our school. Did a demo. Showed us the curriculum. It aligned with what we're already teaching. And they offered us 10% commission on every student who signed up. That helped. We used that money to buy lab equipment."

> "First, is it legitimate? Is it a scam? I checked their registration. Talked to other principals. Second, is it affordable for our parents? Third, will it actually help students? We can't recommend something that doesn't work."

**On competitive dynamics:**
> "If St. Mary's launches an AI program and we don't, parents will ask, 'Why not?' So we have to be seen as keeping up. But carefully. We can't be first. We can't be last. We have to be... smart followers."

**On risk (powerful story):**
> "Three years ago, we recommended a tuition program. The provider disappeared with parents' money. Do you know what that did to my reputation? Parents blamed ME. 'You sent us to them.' I almost lost my job. So now... I'm very careful."

> "If it's not proven. If it's new. If I don't know who's running it. If there's no institution behind it. Parents will come to me if something goes wrong. I need protection."

**On success:**
> "Students came back with skills. They could type, use Word, Excel. Parents were happy. They told other parents. That's how it should work. Success brings more students. Failure... failure destroys trust."

### Jobs-to-be-Done Identified
- **Functional Job:** "Recommend programs that work without risking my reputation"
- **Emotional Job:** "Feel safe that I won't be blamed if something goes wrong"
- **Social Job:** "Be seen as current and forward-thinking (but not experimental)"

### Assumption Validation
| Assumption | Status | Evidence |
|------------|--------|----------|
| Schools want "progressive" signaling | ✅ PARTIAL | **"Smart followers"** — want to be current but NOT first |
| Schools recommend for "altruistic" reasons | ❌ CONTRADICTED | **"10% commission"** — **Financial incentive matters** |
| Principals understand Zone framework | ❌ CONTRADICTED | **"We don't know what to tell parents"** — Framework too abstract |

### Segment Classification
**Segment: Cautious Public Follower** — Large public schools that want proven programs with financial incentives and strong risk mitigation. They want to be current but not experimental.

**💡 CRITICAL INSIGHT:** These schools need:
1. **Track record** (references from other schools)
2. **Financial incentive** (commission/budget share)
3. **Institutional backing** (not individual providers)
4. **Risk protection** (refund policy, insurance)
5. **Alignment** with existing curriculum

## Interview #2: Mrs. Kamau (Age 45, Private Girls' School)

### Persona Profile
- Principal of mid-tier private girls' school (400 students)
- 15 years in education
- Middle to upper-middle income families
- **Core pressures:** Competitive enrollment; parent expectations for advantage
- **Decision mindset:** Competitively driven

### Key Quotes

**On parent demand:**
> "Parents ask about AI constantly. 'Are you teaching it? Will my daughter fall behind?' I tell them we're exploring options. But honestly? I'm overwhelmed by options. Everyone says they have the best AI program. Who do I trust?"

**On decision criteria:**
> "The presenter was impressive. Young, dynamic. The girls were excited. And... the other girls' school in town did it. If they did and we didn't, parents would notice. So we matched them."

> "Testimonials from other schools. Photos of happy students. A clear curriculum. And they trained one of our teachers for free. That teacher now oversees coding club. Win-win."

**On risk:**
> "I turned down a program last year. Why? No track record. New provider. No references. I asked, 'Which schools have you worked with?' They couldn't name any. I said no. Parents trust my judgment. I can't risk that on something unproven."

**On success:**
> "The girls enjoyed it. They came home talking about it. Parents told me, 'My daughter is so excited.' That excitement is valuable. It shows parents we're innovative. It helps with enrollment."

### Jobs-to-be-Done Identified
- **Functional Job:** "Keep up with competitor schools to maintain enrollment"
- **Emotional Job:** "Feel confident I'm making competitive choices for my students"
- **Social Job:** "Signal innovation to prospective parents"

### Assumption Validation
| Assumption | Status | Evidence |
|------------|--------|----------|
| Schools recommend for altruistic reasons | ❌ CONTRADICTED | **"If they did and we didn't, parents would notice"** = Competitive pressure |
| "AI cohort" enhances reputation | ✅ VALIDATED | **"It helps with enrollment"** — Marketing advantage |
| Principals understand Zone framework | ❌ CONTRADICTED | **"I'm overwhelmed by options"** — Needs simplification |

### Segment Classification
**Segment: Competitive Private School** — Mid-tier private schools driven by competitive parity with peer schools. They make recommendations based on what competitor schools are doing and what generates student excitement/parent buzz.

**💡 CRITICAL INSIGHT:** These schools are the **BEST FIT for K2M's current model** because:
1. Belonging/cohort model aligns with their culture
2. Price point (3,500 KES) is accessible to their parents
3. They value "excitement" and "innovation" — K2M delivers both
4. They respond to social proof (other schools doing it)

**Strategy:** Make Mrs. Kamau's school your **pilot partner**. Use their success to attract other private schools.

## Interview #3: Dr. Kiplagat (Age 58, National School - Elite)

### Persona Profile
- Principal of prestigious national school (2,000 students)
- 32 years in education
- Top performers, competitive admission, elite families
- **Core pressures:** Maintain #1 ranking; produce future leaders; stay ahead
- **Budget:** Well-funded, alumni network, parent contributions

### Key Quotes

**On parent expectations:**
> "Parents here don't ask if we're teaching AI. They ask, 'WHICH AI program are you offering?' They expect excellence. They expect us to be ahead. We can't be behind other schools. It's not acceptable."

**On decision criteria:**
> "We partnered with a university last year for an advanced STEM program. Parents paid 25,000 KES. 100 students joined."

> "The university's reputation. Their professors. The rigor. This isn't about basic skills. It's about giving our students an edge. When they apply to Harvard, to Oxford, they need more than good grades. They need distinction."

**On exclusivity:**
> "Excellence. Credentials. Innovation. I don't want a cookie-cutter program. I want something that sets my school apart. If everyone's doing it, why should we?"

**On competition:**
> "I know what other top schools are doing. I track it. If Alliance launches something new, I hear about it immediately. Parents ask me, 'When are we doing that?' So we have to lead, not follow. Or at minimum, match quickly."

**On success:**
> "Success? My students winning competitions. Getting into Ivy League universities. Being featured in the news. That's what parents pay for. That's what builds our brand. A program is successful if it makes us look good."

### Jobs-to-be-Done Identified
- **Functional Job:** "Maintain competitive dominance and produce exceptional outcomes"
- **Emotional Job:** "Feel secure that my school is leading, not following"
- **Social Job:** "Signal exclusivity and prestige to external stakeholders"

### Assumption Validation
| Assumption | Status | Evidence |
|------------|--------|----------|
| Schools want "progressive" signaling | ✅ STRONGLY VALIDATED | **"We have to lead, not follow"** |
| Schools recommend for altruistic reasons | ❌ CONTRADICTED | **"Makes us look good"** — Reputation and branding |
| Principals understand Zone framework | ❌ CONTRADICTED | Wants "distinction," not frameworks |

### Segment Classification
**Segment: Elite Status Leader** — Top-tier national schools focused on exclusivity, prestige, and competitive dominance. They want programs that set them apart, not ones that "everyone" does.

**💡 CRITICAL INSIGHT:** Current K2M model is **NOT a fit** for this segment because:
1. **Too accessible** — mass-market cohort lacks exclusivity
2. **Price too low** — signals "basic" not "elite"
3. **No university partnership** — lacks prestige credibility
4. **Belonging model** — elite students want competitive advantage, not community

**Opportunity:** Create **"K2M Elite"** tier:
- Price: 15,000-25,000 KES
- University partnership (Strathmore/USIU?)
- Application-based selection (maintains exclusivity)
- Focus: Portfolio building, Ivy League prep, competitive differentiation
- Outcome: "Set your child apart"

## Interview #4: Mr. Njoroge (Age 49, Rural Day School)

### Persona Profile
- Principal of rural secondary school (500 students)
- 20 years in education
- Low-income farming community
- **Core pressures:** Limited resources, high dropout rates, basic needs
- **Budget:** Extremely limited; struggles to pay teachers consistently

### Key Quotes

**On AI relevance:**
> "AI? To be honest, that's not our priority. My students don't have computers. Many don't have phones. Some come to school hungry. AI feels like a luxury for city schools."

> "Some parents hear things on radio. But most just want their children to finish school, get decent grades, maybe get a job. AI is...遥远 [far away]."

**On past program (FREE NGO initiative):**
> "Two years ago, an NGO brought a free digital literacy program. They brought laptops. They taught basic computer skills. 100 students participated."

> "It was FREE. That's the main thing. And they brought everything. We didn't have to pay. Our parents couldn't afford 3,500 KES. Most can barely afford exam fees."

**On what would enable participation:**
> "Scholarships. Or a sponsor. Maybe one of the parents who's doing well could sponsor 5 students. Or the government. If the Ministry of Education paid for it, yes. But asking parents for 3,500 KES? That's not realistic here."

**On success metrics:**
> "The free program was successful because students learned something they wouldn't have otherwise. But honestly, I measure success differently. Did fewer students drop out? Did more pass their exams? Did any get scholarships? Those are my metrics. AI doesn't help with those if it's not accessible."

### Jobs-to-be-Done Identified
- **Functional Job:** "Provide any opportunity I can to my students without costing families money"
- **Emotional Job:** "Feel like I'm doing my best despite extreme resource constraints"
- **Social Job:** "Demonstrate to community that the school offers value despite limitations"

### Assumption Validation
| Assumption | Status | Evidence |
|------------|--------|----------|
| Schools want "progressive" signaling | ❌ CONTRADICTED | **"AI feels like a luxury for city schools"** |
| "AI cohort" enhances reputation | ❌ CONTRADICTED | **Basic needs > advanced skills** |
| Principals understand Zone framework | ❌ CONTRADICTED | **Not a priority** |

### Segment Classification
**Segment: Resource-Constrained Basic Needs** — Rural and low-income schools focused on basic education needs. External programs are only viable if fully sponsored.

**💡 CRITICAL INSIGHT:** K2M is **completely inaccessible** to this segment at 3,500 KES. Only path forward:

**Option A: Sponsorship Model**
- Corporate sponsors (Safaricom, Equity Bank?)
- Government partnership (Ministry of Education)
- NGO funding
- One sponsor per 5-10 students

**Option B: Infrastructure-Light Model**
- Phone-based (no laptop required)
- Data stipends included
- Offline-first approach
- **Still requires sponsorship** — parents cannot pay

**Reality:** This segment (30% of Kenyan schools) is **out of reach** for K2M's current model. Consider it a **future Phase 2 opportunity** after establishing revenue in accessible segments.

## Interview #5: Mrs. Chelugui (Age 41, Technical/Vocational Institute)

### Persona Profile
- Principal of technical training institute (800 students)
- Focus: Practical skills, job placement
- Students: Those who didn't qualify for university + upskilling adults
- **Core pressures:** Employment rates, graduate success stories
- **Budget:** Moderate, funded by fees + government TVET money

### Key Quotes

**On employer demand:**
> "Employers ask for computer skills. AI skills? Not yet, but coming. Our mandate is job readiness. If AI is part of that, we should teach it. But I need proof it leads to jobs."

**On decision criteria:**
> "We added a digital marketing course last year. 5,000 KES for 6 weeks. 40 students enrolled."

> "We surveyed employers. What skills do you need? Digital marketing came up. We found a trainer. Students completed the course. Some got jobs using those skills. That's success."

**On what matters:**
> "Employer demand. Job placement data. Practical skills, not theory. Our students are practical. They want to see how this puts food on the table."

**On risk:**
> "I rejected a program last year. Very theoretical. No clear job outcomes. I said, 'My students are struggling. They don't have money to waste on theory.' I need ROI. Will this get them a job? If yes, I'm interested. If no, goodbye."

**On success:**
> "Success is simple: Graduates get jobs. Last year, 70% of our digital marketing students found work using those skills. That's proof. That's what I sell to parents: 'Your child will get a job.' That's all that matters."

### Jobs-to-be-Done Identified
- **Functional Job:** "Place graduates in jobs using skills learned"
- **Emotional Job:** "Feel confident I'm offering practical value, not false promises"
- **Social Job:** "Build reputation as a pathway to employment"

### Assumption Validation
| Assumption | Status | Evidence |
|------------|--------|----------|
| Schools want "progressive" signaling | ❌ CONTRADICTED | **"Employer demand"** drives decisions, not signaling |
| Schools recommend for altruistic reasons | ❌ CONTRADICTED | **"Will this get them a job?"** = Pragmatic ROI |
| Principals understand Zone framework | ❌ CONTRADICTED | **"Practical skills, not theory"** |

### Segment Classification
**Segment: Employment-Focused Technical** — TVET and technical institutes focused on job placement. They make recommendations based on employer demand and proven employment outcomes.

**💡 CRITICAL INSIGHT:** This segment requires a **completely different value proposition**:

**Reframe K2M for Technical Institutes:**
- **Tagline:** "AI Skills That Get You Hired"
- **Focus:** Practical job applications (AI for administrative work, customer service, data entry)
- **Employer partnerships:** Get 5-10 employers to endorse the skills
- **Outcome:** Place graduates in jobs using AI
- **Price:** 5,000-7,000 KES (higher ROI expectation)
- **Curriculum:** Less "thinking habits," more "practical workflows"

**Pilot Opportunity:** Partner with Mrs. Chelugui's institute. Create "AI for Office Work" track. Place 10 graduates in admin jobs using AI skills. Use success to market to other TVETs.

## Cross-Interview Analysis

### Five School Segments with Different Jobs-to-be-Done

| Segment | % of Schools | Primary Job | What "Proof" Means | K2M Fit |
|---------|--------------|-------------|-------------------|---------|
| **Cautious Public** | 40% | "Recommend without risk" | References + commission + track record | ⚠️ Needs risk mitigation |
| **Competitive Private** | 20% | "Keep up with competitors" | Student excitement + parent buzz | ✅ **BEST FIT** |
| **Elite Status Leader** | 5% | "Maintain dominance" | Prestige + exclusivity + Ivy League outcomes | ❌ Needs premium tier |
| **Resource-Constrained** | 30% | "Provide any opportunity free" | FREE + infrastructure | ❌ Price prohibits access |
| **Employment-Focused** | 5% | "Place graduates in jobs" | Job placement data + employer demand | ⚠️ Needs reframing |

### Critical Insights for K2M Strategy

#### 1. **Single-product strategy is misaligned with 80% of schools**

Only **Competitive Private schools (20%)** align with current K2M model. Remaining segments require:

- **Cautious Public (40%):** Commission structure + risk protection + proof of track record
- **Elite Status (5%):** Premium tier + university partnership + exclusivity
- **Resource-Constrained (30%):** Sponsorship model (different business)
- **Employment-Focused (5%):** Job-focused curriculum + employer partnerships

#### 2. **B2B sales process is 6-12 months, not direct-to-consumer speed**

**Mr. Omondi's story:** "Three years ago, provider disappeared. Parents blamed ME. I almost lost my job."

**Implication:** Schools take **6-12 months** to trust and recommend. Sales cycle is:
1. Initial outreach
2. Provide references from other schools
3. Pilot with small group (10-20 students)
4. Measure success
5. Present to board/PTA
6. Full roll-out

**Reality:** Feb 14 launch is **too early for school partnerships** to drive significant enrollment. Focus on direct-to-parent for Cohort 1, build school partnerships for Cohort 2+.

#### 3. **Commission model is expected, not optional**

**Mrs. Kamau:** "They trained one of our teachers for free. Win-win."

**Mr. Omondi:** "They offered us 10% commission. That helped. We used that money to buy lab equipment."

**Recommendation:** Build commission into pricing model:
- **Standard price:** 4,000 KES
- **School price:** 3,000 KES (after 1,000 KES commission)
- **School keeps 1,000 KES** per student
- **OR:** School gets 5 free teacher spots for every 20 paying students

#### 4. **"Zone framework" is too abstract for principals**

All 5 principals showed NO understanding of the Territory Map/Zone framework.

**Recommendation:** Create **Principal-Facing Materials** that translate zones into:
- **"From confusion to clarity"** (simple, emotional)
- **"From 'AI is not for me' to 'I use AI daily'"** (before/after stories)
- **"Zone shifts"** → "skill milestones" (more concrete)
- **Photos/videos** of student transformation (visual proof)

#### 5. **Rural schools represent 30% of market but are inaccessible**

**Mr. Njoroge:** "Our parents couldn't afford 3,500 KES. Most can barely afford exam fees."

**Reality:** This demographic cannot be served by K2M's current business model. Options:
1. **Accept this limitation** and focus on accessible segments
2. **Create sponsored cohort** (seek corporate/government funding)
3. **Develop infrastructure-light model** for future expansion

## Strategic Recommendations

### Immediate Actions (Cohort 1 - Feb 14 Launch)

#### 1. **Focus Direct-to-Parent Marketing First**
- School partnerships take 6-12 months
- Feb 14 launch is too early for B2B to drive enrollment
- **Target:** Competitive Private School parents (Mrs. Kamau's demographic)
- **Channel:** Parent WhatsApp groups, social media ads, targeted Facebook/Instagram

#### 2. **Pilot with ONE Private School**
- Find a "Mrs. Kamau" equivalent
- Offer: Free teacher training + 20% commission + first cohort risk-free
- **Goal:** Create case study for future school sales
- **Deliverable:** Video testimonial + success metrics + parent quotes

#### 3. **Create Principal-Facing One-Pager**
Translate zones into principal-friendly language:
- **Problem:** "Your students are confused about AI. You're unsure what to recommend."
- **Solution:** "6-week program that takes students from 'AI is scary' to 'I use AI confidently'"
- **Proof:** Pilot school results (after first cohort)
- **Offer:** 1,000 KES commission per student OR 5 free teacher spots per 20 students

### Medium-Term (Cohort 2+ - Mid-2026)

#### 4. **Build School Partnership Pipeline**
- Hire B2B salesperson (former teacher/principal?)
- Target: 50 Competitive Private schools for Cohort 2
- Offer: Commission + teacher training + marketing materials
- **Timeline:** Start outreach March 2026 for August cohort

#### 5. **Launch "K2M Elite" Premium Tier**
- Target: Elite Status Leader schools (Dr. Kiplagat's segment)
- Price: 15,000 KES
- Features: University partnership, application-based selection, portfolio focus, Ivy League prep
- **Differentiation:** "Not for everyone. For students who want to lead."

#### 6. **Develop TVET/Employment Track**
- Target: Employment-Focused technical institutes
- Curriculum: Practical AI workflows for office/admin work
- Partnerships: Employer endorsements
- **Outcome:** Job placement guarantee
- **Price:** 5,000-7,000 KES

### Long-Term (2027+)

#### 7. **Create Sponsored Rural Cohort Model**
- Seek corporate sponsors (CSR programs)
- Government partnership (Ministry of Education, TVET authority)
- NGOs with education focus
- **Model:** Sponsor pays 3,000 KES, student pays 500 KES (commitment)
